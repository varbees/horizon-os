# The Horizon OS Startup Playbook

This is the canonical knowledge base for evaluating and structuring projects within Horizon OS. Every active project must be mapped against these frameworks in the `/strategy` module.

## 1. Market Sizing (TAM, SAM, SOM)
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** The absolute universe of revenue if you achieved 100% market share. It proves the ceiling. (e.g., Global Wedding Industry = $300B).
- **SAM (Serviceable Available Market):** The portion of TAM you can realistically serve with your current product and business model. (e.g., Indian Wedding Photography Software = $50M).
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** The realistic chunk of SAM you can capture in the next 1-3 years. This is your immediate target. (e.g., 5% of mid-tier Indian studios = $2.5M).

## 2. Beachhead Market
The narrowest, most specific subset of customers where your product is a "must-have" rather than a "nice-to-have." You must dominate this beachhead before expanding. 
*Example:* Instead of "all photographers," start with "High-volume Indian wedding studios in Tier-2 cities doing 50+ events a year who struggle with WhatsApp payment follow-ups."

## 3. Moats & Defensibility
- **Network Effects:** The product becomes more valuable to existing users as new users join (e.g., Marketplaces).
- **Switching Costs:** The financial, operational, or emotional pain a user experiences when leaving your product (e.g., deeply integrated API systems or locked-in data).
- **Competitive Advantage:** What you do fundamentally better than anyone else. It must be structural (proprietary tech, unique access, capital efficiency), not just "we try harder."
- **Barriers to Entry:** What prevents a well-funded competitor from cloning your product in 3 months? (e.g., complex compliance, heavy upfront hardware costs, entrenched brand trust).

## 4. Market Strategy
- **Market Timing:** Why now? What technological, regulatory, or social shift makes this possible today that wasn't possible 3 years ago?
- **Category Creation:** Are you fighting for a slice of an existing pie, or inventing a new category? (e.g., "Revenue Collection Engine" instead of "Photo Sharing App").
- **Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean:** 
  - *Red Ocean:* Competing in an existing market space, beating the competition on price/features (bloody waters).
  - *Blue Ocean:* Creating uncontested market space, making the competition irrelevant.

## 5. Business Models
- **Vertical vs. Horizontal SaaS:** 
  - *Vertical:* Built for a specific industry (e.g., software just for dentists or just for wedding photographers). High penetration, low churn.
  - *Horizontal:* Built for a specific function across all industries (e.g., Slack, Notion). Huge TAM, brutal competition.
- **Platform Models:** Creating the underlying infrastructure upon which others build value.
- **Marketplaces:** Connecting supply and demand. Hard to bootstrap due to the "chicken and egg" problem, but highly defensible once scaled.
- **Aggregators:** Controlling demand by organizing scattered supply (e.g., Google, Uber, Zomato).

*Rule of execution: Never build a feature until you know which of these levers it strengthens.*
